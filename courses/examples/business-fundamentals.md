# Course: Business Fundamentals

**Target learner:** Secondary school student (ages 15–18) taking a business studies course. Little or no real-world business experience, but curious and able to handle simple math.
**Goal:** Understand how a business actually works financially — from what it sells to whether it makes money.
**Suggested session length:** 30 min

---

## Modules

### Module 1: What is a business?
**Outcome:** Explain what a business is, what it does, and the main types (sole trader, partnership, company).
**Key concepts:** purpose of a business, goods vs services, types of business structures, stakeholders
**Teaching notes:** Start from examples the learner knows — a local café, a clothing brand they wear, a YouTuber selling merch. Keep it concrete. Avoid legal jargon.

### Module 2: Revenue, costs, and profit
**Outcome:** Calculate profit from revenue and costs, and explain the difference between the three.
**Key concepts:** revenue (sales), costs (what you spend), profit = revenue − costs, why profit matters
**Teaching notes:** Use a simple scenario — selling homemade cookies at school. Walk through one week of sales and spending. Let them do the arithmetic.

### Module 3: Fixed vs variable costs
**Outcome:** Sort a list of costs into fixed and variable, and explain why the distinction matters.
**Key concepts:** fixed costs (rent, salaries), variable costs (materials, packaging), how variable costs scale with output
**Teaching notes:** This is the setup for break-even. Make sure they can confidently sort examples before moving on. Common confusion: staff wages — is it fixed or variable? Depends on contract. Explore that.

### Module 4: Break-even analysis
**Outcome:** Calculate the break-even point for a simple business and explain what it means for decision-making.
**Key concepts:** contribution per unit, break-even formula (fixed costs ÷ contribution per unit), margin of safety, what break-even tells you about risk
**Teaching notes:** Don't start with the formula. Start with a story: "You want to sell T-shirts at a school fair. Each shirt costs you €8 to make, you sell them for €15, and the stall costs €70. How many do you need to sell before you make any money?" Let them work through it. Then introduce the formula as a shortcut for what they just did.

### Module 5: Pricing strategies
**Outcome:** Explain cost-plus, competitive, and value-based pricing — and pick one for a given scenario.
**Key concepts:** markup, market positioning, price elasticity (intuitive, not formal)
**Teaching notes:** Use real brands. Why is a Rolex expensive? Why are Ryanair flights cheap? Let them reason.

### Module 6: Cash flow basics
**Outcome:** Explain why a profitable business can still run out of money, and read a simple cash flow table.
**Key concepts:** cash in vs cash out, timing, the difference between profit and cash
**Teaching notes:** Classic pitfall — students confuse profit with cash. Use an example where a business is profitable on paper but can't pay its bills this month.

### Module 7: Reading a simple P&L
**Outcome:** Read a basic profit and loss statement and spot whether the business is healthy.
**Key concepts:** revenue, cost of sales, gross profit, operating costs, net profit
**Teaching notes:** Give a real-ish P&L from a small fictional business. Ask what stands out. Don't over-explain accounting — the goal is literacy, not expertise.

---

## Adaptation notes

- **Already comfortable with basic math and business terms:** Compress modules 1–2 into a quick check. Spend time on modules 4 and 6 — these are the conceptually tricky ones.
- **Struggles with arithmetic:** Slow down in module 2. Use a calculator openly — the goal is the concept, not mental math.
- **Interested in entrepreneurship specifically:** After module 4, let them sketch break-even for a business idea they have.
